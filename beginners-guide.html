A Beginner's Guide to Resistance Training

By Ashton Valeroso

This guide is designed to help you train effectively and build strength and muscle—even if you're just starting out. If you've ever wanted to develop a consistent workout habit or step into the gym with confidence, this guide will explain the basic terms and concepts you need to get started. Remember, even small amounts of resistance training can have big benefits for your health, mood, and overall well-being.

For many, starting a fitness journey is about more than building strength - it's about building a stronger version of yourself. Fitness develops discipline, confidence, and resilience, qualities that carry over into every part of life. By taking the first steps in the gym, you're also laying the foundation for lasting self-improvement and positive change to your life.

What Is Resistance Training?

Resistance training (also called strength training or weight training) is any form of exercise where your muscles work against a force or resistance. The goal is to make your muscles stronger and promote growth over time.

Some examples of resistance training include

  • Free weights (dumbbells, barbells, kettlebells)
  • Machines (cable machines, leg press, chest press)
  • Bodyweight (push-ups, pull-ups, squats)
  • Bands (resistance bands providing elastic tension)

Resistance training works by creating small tears in your muscle fibers. When you rest and recover, your body repairs these fibers, making them stronger and sometimes larger. This process is called muscle hypertrophy.


How to Set Training Goals?

Why Is Resistance Training Important?

Resistance training is essential for maintaining and improving overall health. It not only builds muscle and strength but also combats the natural loss of muscle mass that typically begins around age 30 and can accelerate through middle age and beyond.

Key Benefits:

● Builds muscle and strength

● Improves bone density and joint health

● Boosts metabolism and supports fat loss

● Enhances balance, coordination, and posture

● Supports better mood by reducing stress and releasing “feel-good” endorphins

● Improves sleep quality and energy levels

● Promotes long-term health, mobility, and independence

How to Set Training Goals?

Everyone's fitness journey is unique, but many people share similar motivations—whether it's to build muscle, gain strength, improve health, enhance performance in a sport, or simply feel more confident. The key is turning these broad ideas into clear, achievable goals that keep you motivated over time.

Start by defining what matters most to you. Ask yourself:

● Do I want to focus on building strength, endurance, or muscle size?

● Am I training for health and longevity? For performance in a specific sport?

● What kind of progress will keep me motivated—physical appearance, strength numbers, or overall energy and wellness?

Once you've identified your priorities, use the SMART framework (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) to set goals you can realistically work toward. For example, instead of saying “I want to get stronger”, try “I want to increase my squat by 20 pounds in the next 3 months.”

What are Reps?

Reps (short for repetitions) are one complete movement of an exercise.

● Example: Lowering and pressing the barbell once in a bench press counts as 1 rep.

● Doing 10 push-ups means you've completed 10 reps.

What are Sets?

Sets are a group of reps performed consecutively before resting.

● Example: Doing 10 push-ups in a row, then resting, equals 1 set of 10 reps.

● If you repeat that three times, you've done 3 sets of 10 reps, usually written as 3×10.

Sets help organize your training by breaking reps into manageable chunks, with rest in between to maximize performance.

What are Rep Ranges?

A rep range is the number of repetitions you perform in a single set of an exercise. According to this study, different rep ranges are used to target different training goals:

● Strength: 3–6 reps per set (heavier weight, fewer reps). For testing maximal strength, 1–3 reps per set may be used.

● Hypertrophy (Muscle Growth): 8–12 reps per set (moderate weight). This is the most commonly recommended range for most exercises, balancing safety, intensity, and effort close to muscle fatigue.

● Endurance: 12–20+ reps per set (lighter weight, higher reps). Often used for smaller or stubborn muscle groups, or when heavier resistance is not feasible.

How Many Sets Should I Do?

The number of sets you perform depends on your training goal. The amount of sets determines how many exercises you should incorporate into your routine. Compound exercises can be used to work multiple muscles at once to reduce the total amount of exercises you perform per workout.

● A general guideline is to aim for at least 6–8 sets per muscle group per week to maintain or modestly grow muscle.

● Performing 10–20 sets per muscle group per week is ideal for maximizing muscle growth.

In general, 3 solid working sets per exercise is usually the default number of sets that I tend to follow. Other fitness advice suggests a certain number of sets depending on your training goal.

● Strength: 3–6 sets per exercise

● Hypertrophy (Muscle Growth): 3–4 sets per exercise

● Endurance: 2–3 sets per exercise

How Much Rest Time Should I Have Between Sets?

Rest periods depend on the intensity and goal of your exercise. Generally, around 2 minutes is recommended for adequate recovery and optimal performance. I would usually recommend at least 2 minutes of rest for heavy strength exercises and 90 seconds to 2 minutes for hypertrophy exercises. It is always better to rest more than less for maximizing performance for each set.

By Goal:

● Strength: 2–5 minutes — longer rest allows full recovery for heavy lifting.

● Hypertrophy (Muscle Growth): 30–90 seconds — shorter rest keeps muscles under tension to promote growth.

● Endurance: 15–60 seconds — maintains heart rate and supports stamina development.

How Much Weight Should I Use?

The weight you choose should align with your training goal and how close you want to get to muscular fatigue. For beginners, a good starting point is a weight you can lift with proper form for 3 sets of 8–12 reps. Once the last few reps of each set feel manageable, gradually increase the weight using progressive overload while maintaining control and proper technique. As you gain experience, it becomes easier to gauge the right weight to reach near-failure within different rep ranges, helping you maximize strength and muscle growth safely.

What is Progressive Overload?

Progressive overload is the key principle behind getting stronger and building muscle. It means gradually increasing the demands placed on your body so it continues to adapt. Without progressive overload, your muscles, joints, and nervous system have no reason to grow stronger, and your progress will eventually stall.

There are several effective ways to apply progressive overload:

● Increase the Weight – The most common method is to slowly add more weight to the bar, dumbbell, or machine once you can complete your sets with solid form.

● Add More Reps or Sets – If you're not ready to increase weight, aim to complete extra reps or add another set while maintaining good technique.

● Progress to Harder Variations – Move from easier to more challenging exercise variations (e.g., push-ups to decline push-ups).

● Improve Your Form – Lifting with tighter control and better range of motion increases the difficulty and effectiveness of an exercise without adding weight.

● Manipulate Tempo – Slowing down the lowering phase, pausing at the bottom, or controlling the speed of each rep increases time under tension and forces your muscles to adapt.

A good rule of thumb is to apply progressive overload every 1–3 sessions for a given exercise, depending on your recovery and training level. Moving too quickly can lead to sloppy technique or injury, so focus on small, consistent improvements rather than big jumps. By following progressive overload carefully, you'll build strength, improve endurance, and see steady progress while minimizing the risk of setbacks.

Safety and Proper Technique

Safety and proper technique are the foundation of effective training. Without them, you not only risk injury but also limit your ability to make progress in the long run. Good form ensures that the right muscles are targeted, the joints are protected, and the body learns to move in strong, efficient patterns. On the other hand, poor technique and unsafe habits—like rushing through reps or lifting too heavy too soon—can lead to setbacks, pain, or even long-term damage.

Here are some key principles to practice:

● Master Proper Form First – Before adding weight, learn the correct technique for every exercise. Compound lifts like squats, deadlifts, and bench press especially require precise mechanics to protect your back, knees, and shoulders. Watching tutorials, recording yourself, or asking an experienced lifter or coach for feedback can help.

● Use Controlled Movements – Avoid bouncing or jerking weights. Controlled eccentrics (the lowering phase) not only reduce injury risk but also build more strength and muscle.

● Lift an Appropriate Weight – Choose a weight you can handle with proper form. If your technique breaks down, the weight is too heavy. Quality always comes before quantity.

● Be Aware of Your Surroundings – In a busy gym, pay attention to where you set equipment and who is nearby to avoid accidents.

● Listen to Your Body – Sharp pain, dizziness, or unusual discomfort are signs to stop immediately. Muscle fatigue is normal; joint or sharp pain is not.

● Ask for a Spotter When Needed – For heavy presses or squats, having a spotter provides safety and confidence so you can train harder without unnecessary risk.

● Warm Up and Cool Down – A proper warm-up prepares your muscles and joints for movement, while stretching or mobility work afterward aids recovery and prevents stiffness.

Practicing safety and proper technique consistently will not only keep you injury-free but also allow you to train harder, progress faster, and build a stronger foundation over time.

Consistency

Consistency is the most important factor in making progress with resistance training. Your body adapts over time to repeated effort, and lasting results come from showing up and training regularly—not from one perfect workout. Muscular growth and strength improvements depend on how consistently you apply effort, not just how intensely you train on occasion.

Building consistency starts with creating habits:

● Set a Realistic Schedule – Choose training days and times that fit your lifestyle so you can stick with them long term.

● Train Every Major Muscle Group – Balance is key; working all muscle groups regularly helps prevent weaknesses and supports overall growth.

● Focus on Progress, Not Perfection – Missing a day won't ruin your progress. What matters is returning to your routine and staying on track over weeks, months, and years.

● Stay Patient – Strength and muscle gains are gradual. Small improvements each session add up to significant results over time.

Think of consistency as the foundation of your fitness journey. Without it, even the best program won't deliver results. With it, progress becomes inevitable.

What should I do before I work out?

Before exercising, it's best to do a light warm-up that includes dynamic stretching to prepare your joints and muscles. Movements like leg swings, arm circles, or shoulder rotations help increase blood flow and mobility. If you're performing a difficult exercise or a heavy compound lift such as the squat, bench press, or deadlift, start with a few lighter sets of the same exercise. This primes your body for proper form and performance without tiring you out before your main working sets.

What should I do after I work out?

After resistance training, it's recommended to include static stretching and flexibility work. This is a good way to help you cool down and relax after a difficult workout. This will help you reduce the amount of stress that you have to help you cognitively for the rest of the day. This helps your muscles relax, supports recovery, and may even provide additional benefits for long-term muscle growth and mobility.

How do I incorporate cardio into my training?

For overall health, it is recommended to aim for at least 150 minutes of zone 2 cardio (light to moderate effort) each week, such as brisk walking, easy cycling, or a steady jog where you can still hold a conversation. Dedicated cardio sessions in the gym are not necessary if you already live a very active lifestyle. In addition, including one weekly session of zone 5 cardio (high-intensity effort), such as sprints, HIIT intervals, or hill runs, is recommended to support heart health.

It is recommended to do cardio exercises on separate days as active rest days or after a resistance training workout. It is not recommended to train cardio before resistance training because this could result in poor performance and higher chances of injury.

Proper Breathing

Breathing technique is often overlooked, but it can make a big difference in performance and safety. For resistance training, a good rule of thumb is to inhale during the eccentric phase (lowering the weight) and exhale during the concentric phase (lifting the weight). In general, exhale during the hardest part of the exercise and inhale during the easier part.

For cardio, breathing patterns vary depending on intensity. During low-intensity cardio, aim for steady, controlled breaths. During high-intensity sessions, your breathing will naturally become faster, but focus on maintaining rhythm rather than gasping.

The Importance of Diet

Training on its own won't deliver maximum results - nutrition is essential for fueling performance, aiding recovery, and supporting muscle growth and fat loss. The food you eat provides the energy and building blocks your body needs to adapt, both physically and mentally.

● Muscle Growth: To build muscle, you generally need a slight caloric surplus (200-500 calories above maintenance).

● Fat Loss or Recomposition: For fat loss, aim for a slight caloric deficit (200-500 calories below maintenance) while keeping protein intake high to preserve strength and muscle.

● Protein Intake: A common guideline is about 1 gram of protein per pound of body weight per day, though anywhere from 0.7–1 g/lb is generally effective.

● Balanced Nutrition: Not all calories are equal—nutrient-dense foods that provide the proper amounts of macronutrients, vitamins, minerals, healthy fats, and fiber will support performance and long-term health.

● Calorie Awareness: Using a calorie calculator can help estimate daily needs. Tracking your intake for a week or so gives insight into your habits without requiring obsessive long-term logging.

The Importance of Sleep

Just as important as diet, sleep is when much of the recovery and growth process happens. Without adequate rest, training results will stall, no matter how hard you work in the gym.

● Recovery & Growth: During sleep, the body repairs muscle fibers, consolidates memory, and releases key hormones that are essential for recovery.

● Sleep Duration: Most adults should aim for 7–9 hours of quality sleep per night. Athletes or individuals training at high intensity may benefit from more.

● Performance Impact: Poor sleep can impair strength, endurance, reaction time, and cognitive ability. It also disrupts the circadian rhythm, which can negatively affect hormone regulation and appetite.

How to Manage Recovery / Rest Days

For optimal performance and muscle recovery, allow at least 48 hours of rest before training the same muscle group again. This generally means 2–3 days between resistance sessions targeting the same muscles. Proper rest helps prevent overtraining, reduces injury risk, and supports strength and muscle growth.

Conclusion

Starting a resistance training program is more than just lifting weights—it's about building strength, health, and confidence over time. The foundation of progress lies in combining consistent effort, proper technique, and gradual progression. By choosing the right weight, focusing on correct form, and applying progressive overload safely, your body can adapt effectively while minimizing the risk of injury.

Consistency in your routine reinforces these principles. Training regularly and targeting all major muscle groups ensures balanced development, while tracking your progress allows you to see tangible results. Pairing your workouts with adequate nutrition, proper recovery, and sufficient sleep supports your body's ability to repair, grow, and perform at its best. Even simple habits like warming up, cooling down, and stretching can make a meaningful difference in long-term performance and mobility.

Progress happens gradually, and small, deliberate improvements—whether adding weight, completing extra reps, or refining your technique—accumulate over time. Setbacks and challenges are normal, but staying mindful of your technique, recovery, and overall routine keeps you moving forward safely.

By following these strategies, you'll build a strong foundation for long-term results, making steady gains in strength and fitness while developing a sustainable routine. Over time, this approach not only maximizes the benefits of your workouts but also establishes habits that support your health, resilience, and confidence for years to come.
